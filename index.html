Explore how citations flow between articles in the COCI dataset



OpenCitations is an independent not-for-profit infrastructure organization for open scholarship dedicated to the publication of open bibliographic and citation data by the use of Semantic Web (Linked Data) technologies. It is also engaged in advocacy for open citations, particularly in its role as a key founding member of the Initiative for Open Citations (I4OC). For administrative convenience, OpenCitations is managed by the Research Centre for Open Scholarly Metadata at the University of Bologna.

OpenCitations espouses fully the founding principles of Open Science. It complies with the FAIR data principles by Force11 that data should be findable, accessible, interoperable and re-usable, and it complies with the recommendations of I4OC that citation data in particular should be structured, separable, and open. On the latter topic, OpenCitations has recently published a formal definition of an Open Citation, and has launched a system for globally unique and persistent identifiers (PIDs) for bibliographic citations Open Citation Identifiers (OCIs).

COCI in a nutshell

COCI is a very large dataset containing millions of individual items, represented as DOIs, and accounts for more than a bilion citations. Here we are analysing the DOIs that represent scientific articles and are published in academic journals.

COCI Composition: Groups and SuperGroups

COCI is huge and it containes articles belonging to more than 300 different fields. To have a general idea of the composition of COCI, we should look at the major area subdivision, which distinguishes between Physical Sciences, Health Sciences, Life Sciences, Social Sciences and Multidisciplinary. Those 5 groups are also divided in smaller sub-groups, which can be fully explored with the nested pie-chart above, which illustrates the composition of COCI in terms of the groups and sub-groups.

COCI Composition: Fields

Goign more in depth in the areas subdivision, fields are the most fine-graned distinction we can make in COCI. It is interesting to observe how scientific journals can be distinguished into separated academic areas that identify the subjct of the interest of that particular publication. Self Citations and Disciplinary Boundaries

However, it is more interesting to see how subject areas interact between each other. The first thing to observe, in fact, is whether they tend to interact more with fields that are smilar to themselves or whether they are prone to cross disciplary boundaries. This pie chart display the portion of publications in COCI that have mentioned articles belonging to the very same field (self citations), to a similar field e.g. belonging to the same group (partial-self citations) or articles that have no subject area in common (not-self citations).

Citations flow

Citations are links that express a relantioship between two elements. Thus, they represent connections betweeb articles and, in turn, between subject areas. In this diagram you can explore how citations flow from one academic area to another: the width of the connection represents the number of citations that were made from A to B, while colours identify the subject area.
